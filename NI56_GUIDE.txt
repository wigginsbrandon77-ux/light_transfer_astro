NICKEL-56 RADIOACTIVE DECAY HEATING
====================================

The radiative transfer code now includes Ni-56 radioactive decay as an
energy source. This is crucial for modeling supernova light curves!

PHYSICS BACKGROUND
------------------

In supernova explosions, nickel-56 is synthesized in the hot, dense core.
Its decay provides the primary energy source for the optical light curve:

Decay chain:
  Ni-56 → Co-56 → Fe-56
  (6.1 days) (77 days)

Energy release:
  Ni-56 decay: 1.75 MeV per decay
  Co-56 decay: 3.61 MeV per decay

The gamma rays and positrons from these decays thermalize in the ejecta,
heating the gas and powering the luminosity.

Typical values:
  Type Ia supernovae: ~0.6 M_sun of Ni-56
  Core-collapse SNe:  ~0.01-0.2 M_sun of Ni-56

IMPLEMENTATION
--------------

The model includes:

1. TIME-DEPENDENT LUMINOSITY
   L(t) = L_Ni(t) + L_Co(t)
   
   Where:
   L_Ni(t) = (M_Ni * ε_Ni / τ_Ni) * exp(-t/τ_Ni)
   L_Co(t) = (M_Co(t) * ε_Co / τ_Co) * exp(-t/τ_Co)
   
   M_Co(t) grows as Ni-56 decays

2. SPATIAL DISTRIBUTION
   Ni-56 is confined within a user-specified radius
   Typically in the inner ejecta (r < 0.3 R_star)

3. ENERGY DEPOSITION
   Energy is deposited uniformly in the Ni-56 region
   Assumes full gamma-ray trapping (optically thick)
   Increases temperature of heated cells

4. TEMPERATURE UPDATE
   Heating rate → Temperature increase
   dE/dt = heating_rate * cell_mass
   dT = (2/3) * dE / (n k V)

USAGE
-----

Basic usage:
    ./radtransfer output.vtk spectrum.txt -ni56 0.6

Full options:
    ./radtransfer output.vtk spectrum.txt \
        -ni56 0.6 \           # Ni-56 mass in M_sun
        -ni56_radius 0.3 \    # Radius in code units
        -time 20              # Days since explosion

COMMAND-LINE OPTIONS
--------------------

-ni56 <mass>
    Enable Ni-56 decay heating
    Specify initial Ni-56 mass in solar masses
    Example: -ni56 0.6  (typical for Type Ia SN)

-ni56_radius <radius>
    Radius containing Ni-56 in code units
    Default: 0.3
    Example: -ni56_radius 0.2  (more centrally concentrated)

-time <days>
    Time since explosion in days
    Default: 10
    Example: -time 30  (one month after explosion)

EXAMPLES
--------

Example 1: Type Ia supernova at peak light (~20 days)
    ./radtransfer output_000050.vtk spectrum_20d.txt \
        -ni56 0.6 -time 20 -opacity gray

Example 2: Core-collapse SN early time
    ./radtransfer output_000030.vtk spectrum_5d.txt \
        -ni56 0.1 -time 5 -ni56_radius 0.2

Example 3: Time series to track light curve
    for day in 5 10 20 30 50 100; do
        ./radtransfer output.vtk spectrum_${day}d.txt \
            -ni56 0.6 -time $day -opacity gray
    done

Example 4: Different Ni-56 masses
    ./radtransfer output.vtk spec_low_ni56.txt -ni56 0.1 -time 20
    ./radtransfer output.vtk spec_high_ni56.txt -ni56 1.0 -time 20

EXPECTED EFFECTS
----------------

1. TEMPERATURE INCREASE
   Ni-56 heating increases temperature in the inner ejecta
   Higher temperatures → more UV/optical emission
   Peak shifts to shorter wavelengths (bluer)

2. LUMINOSITY EVOLUTION
   Early times (< 10 days):
   - Rapid rise as ejecta becomes transparent
   - Ni-56 decay dominates
   
   Peak (~15-20 days for Type Ia):
   - Maximum light output
   - L_peak ~ 10^43 erg/s for 0.6 M_sun Ni-56
   
   Late times (> 30 days):
   - Co-56 decay dominates (longer half-life)
   - Slower decline
   - Exponential tail

3. SPECTRAL CHANGES
   With Ni-56 heating:
   - Spectrum shifts to higher frequencies (bluer)
   - Increased flux in UV/optical
   - Wien peak at higher frequency
   
   Without Ni-56 heating:
   - Ejecta cools adiabatically
   - Redder colors
   - Lower total luminosity

PHYSICAL INTERPRETATION
-----------------------

Decay luminosity vs. time:

Days   Ni-56 (0.6 M_sun)   Co-56 contribution
  1    1.2e43 erg/s        negligible
  5    9.8e42 erg/s        5%
 10    7.7e42 erg/s        15%
 20    4.8e42 erg/s        35%
 50    1.5e42 erg/s        65%
100    3.0e41 erg/s        85%

The transition from Ni-56 to Co-56 dominance occurs around 20-30 days,
which is why supernova light curves have a characteristic shape:
- Fast rise to peak (Ni-56)
- Slower decline (transition)
- Exponential tail (Co-56)

GAMMA-RAY TRAPPING
------------------

Current implementation:
  - Assumes full trapping (f_trap = 1)
  - All decay energy deposited locally
  - Valid for optically thick ejecta

For more sophisticated models:
  - Trapping fraction: f_trap = 1 - exp(-τ_gamma)
  - τ_gamma depends on density, composition, velocity
  - Gamma rays can escape if τ < 1

At late times or low densities, gamma-ray escape reduces heating
This is not yet implemented but could be added.

COMPARING WITH AND WITHOUT NI-56
---------------------------------

To see the effect of Ni-56 heating:

# Without Ni-56 (adiabatic cooling only)
./radtransfer output.vtk spectrum_no_ni56.txt -opacity gray

# With Ni-56
./radtransfer output.vtk spectrum_with_ni56.txt -ni56 0.6 -time 20 -opacity gray

Compare:
- Total flux (luminosity)
- Peak frequency (color temperature)
- Spectral shape

The Ni-56 heated spectrum should be:
- Brighter (higher flux)
- Bluer (peak at higher frequency)
- Similar shape (still thermal)

VALIDATION
----------

Check if your results make sense:

1. Luminosity scale
   For 0.6 M_sun Ni-56 at 20 days:
   L ~ 5×10^42 erg/s
   
   Compare to your spectrum integrated flux

2. Temperature increase
   Ni-56 heating should raise temperature by ~few×10^3 K
   Check temperature before/after in diagnostics

3. Time dependence
   Luminosity should decrease with time
   L(20 days) > L(50 days) > L(100 days)

4. Mass dependence
   L ∝ M_Ni (to first order)
   Double Ni-56 mass → roughly double luminosity

COMBINING WITH OPACITY MODELS
------------------------------

Ni-56 heating works with all opacity models:

Gray opacity:
  Best for seeing thermal peak
  ./radtransfer output.vtk spectrum.txt -ni56 0.6 -opacity gray

Kramers opacity:
  Physically motivated for stellar interiors
  ./radtransfer output.vtk spectrum.txt -ni56 0.6 -opacity kramers

Free-free opacity:
  For ionized ejecta
  ./radtransfer output.vtk spectrum.txt -ni56 0.6 -opacity freefree

TROUBLESHOOTING
---------------

Problem: "Luminosity seems too high/low"
→ Check Ni-56 mass is reasonable (0.01-1.0 M_sun)
→ Check time since explosion
→ Verify density structure in simulation

Problem: "Temperature increases too much"
→ Check ni56_radius - should be in dense inner ejecta
→ Try smaller Ni-56 mass
→ Check if cells in Ni-56 region have reasonable density

Problem: "No visible effect from Ni-56"
→ Time might be too late (>100 days, decay weakens)
→ Ni-56 mass might be too small
→ Check that ni56_radius intersects dense material

Problem: "Want more realistic trapping"
→ Current model assumes full trapping
→ For advanced use, modify ni56_heating_rate() function
→ Calculate optical depth and use f_trap = 1 - exp(-τ)
